\chapter{Results and Discussion}
\label{results}
The chapter is organized in nine sections that together address the five sub-questions set out in the introduction. The first section places the study's aggregate compute and emissions budget in context. The second establishes the hardware-corrected measurement baseline on which all subsequent comparisons rest. The third quantifies the tuning overhead that standard benchmark tables omit. The main analysis proceeds in four thematic blocks: the cross-dataset benchmark, the \gls{cpu}--\gls{gpu} hardware comparison, the HIGGS scaling and \gls{mlp} architecture variation experiments, and finally the temporal and lifecycle analyses that extend the efficiency picture beyond training time alone.


% ── Aggregate Footprint ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Aggregate Experimental Footprint}
\label{sec:results_scope}

Across 737 CodeCarbon-tracked runs conducted between March and June 2026, the experiments in this study consumed approximately 17.3\,\gls{kwh} of electricity and 255 hours of compute time, with CodeCarbon logging a cumulative 6{,}550\,\gls{gco2eq}, equivalent to driving approximately 55\,km in an average German car or charging a smartphone roughly 860 times on the German grid, computed using a fleet-average factor of 118\,\gls{gco2eq}/km \parencite{europeanenvironmentagencyMonitoringCO2Emissions2025} and an assumed charge energy of 20\,Wh at a grid intensity of 381\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh}. Given the systematic underestimation documented in \Cref{sec:results_measurement}, the corrected total is substantially higher. The 6{,}550\,\gls{gco2eq} should be read as a lower bound. Hyperparameter tuning for the \gls{mlp} on HIGGS alone accounts for 4{,}325\,\gls{gco2eq} of that figure, approximately two thirds of the logged total, illustrating that the experimental overhead of finding a competitive configuration can dwarf the cost of the benchmark runs themselves.

The distribution of cost is highly uneven when viewed by run type rather than by count. The 40 tuning runs across all models and datasets represent just 5\,\% of all logged runs, yet are responsible for 69\,\% of the study's total emissions and 67\,\% of total compute time. The 14 model-dataset combinations that form the core benchmark, each measured as a single training run with already-optimized hyperparameters, account for only a small fraction of the ecological investment required to produce them. This asymmetry is not an anomaly of the experimental design but a structural feature of how \gls{ml} research incurs cost: the visible artifact, the final trained model, represents only the tip of an iceberg whose bulk lies in the iterative search that precedes it.


% ── Measurement Validation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Emissions Measurement Validation}
\label{sec:results_measurement}

Any comparison of model efficiencies is only as valid as the measurements it rests on. As described in \Cref{implementation_emissions}, CodeCarbon's \gls{tdp}-based \gls{cpu} estimate was replaced in this study by direct hardware-sensor readings. \Cref{fig:codecarbon_vs_hw} shows the ratio between the two for every model-dataset combination.

\begin{figure}[htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{codecarbon_vs_hw.pdf}
\caption[CodeCarbon underestimation factor.]{CodeCarbon underestimation factor (hardware-corrected\,/\,CodeCarbon) per model and dataset. All values exceed~1, confirming systematic undercounting. Higher factors for Wine and Credit reflect \gls{tdp}-based estimation being least accurate on short training runs. \gls{rf} has no HIGGS measurement.}
\label{fig:codecarbon_vs_hw}
\end{figure}

Every one of the 14 model-dataset combinations shows a hardware-corrected value that exceeds the raw CodeCarbon recordings. The ratio ranges from 1.30 to 2.63 with a median of 1.92, meaning CodeCarbon, as deployed without modification, would have captured on average only about 52\,\% of the true emissions value. \citeauthor{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} \parencite{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} found a consistent underestimation of 20 to 30\% when validating CodeCarbon against external power meters across a range of workloads. The \gls{cpu}-intensive runs in this study exceed even that range. \gls{lr} on Wine reaches a ratio of 2.59, meaning CodeCarbon captured less than 39\,\% of the actual energy. The cause is the \gls{tdp} fallback described in \Cref{implementation_emissions}. Where \gls{rapl} is unavailable, CodeCarbon estimates \gls{cpu} power from the thermal design envelope rather than measuring it and that envelope does not track how the chip actually behaves under load \parencite{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020}.

The error is not uniformly distributed, and two structural factors account for most of the variation. Short runs are hit hardest. Wine combinations, where training times range from 0.01 to 2.3 seconds, show a mean ratio of 2.24, while HIGGS combinations (16 to 4{,}754 seconds) land at 1.50. On a sub-second run the \gls{tdp}-based integrator barely accumulates anything while the chip executes a real burst of work. As duration grows and load sustains, the heuristic becomes a closer approximation and the ratio shrinks. The \gls{gpu} share is the second factor. \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} on HIGGS, where the \gls{gpu} accounts for 61\,\% of total energy and the \gls{cpu} for only 30\,\%, produces the lowest ratio at 1.30. Since the correction touches only the \gls{cpu} slice, a smaller \gls{cpu} fraction produces a smaller total distortion even when the underlying power discrepancy is comparable to other runs. \citeauthor{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} \parencite{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} document the same asymmetry, finding that \gls{cpu}-only workloads showed errors exceeding \gls{gpu} equivalents by more than an order of magnitude in some configurations.

The measurement error is largest for the runs that correspond to the models Green \gls{ai} positions as the efficient choice. \gls{lr} on Wine carries a ratio of 2.59, \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu} on Credit 2.63. These are the models a practitioner would reach for to minimize ecological footprint, and they are precisely the ones where \gls{tdp}-based estimation fails most severely. Without correction, they would appear even more energy-efficient relative to the heavier workloads than the corrected figures show, because the measurement error and the efficiency claim point in the same direction. The efficiency advantage of lightweight models is confirmed by the corrected data, but the gap is smaller than uncorrected measurements would suggest. All emissions values in the remainder of this chapter use the hardware-corrected figures. The relative rankings are unchanged, but the absolute scale is accurate. Published studies relying on uncorrected CodeCarbon values on comparable hardware should be read as reporting lower bounds rather than ground truth.


% ── Tuning Overhead ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Hyperparameter Tuning Overhead}
\label{sec:results_tuning}

Benchmark tables that compare training emissions report only a fraction of a model's true ecological initialization cost. Before a single benchmark run is executed, each model must be brought to a competitive configuration through hyperparameter search and the footprint of that search is never reflected in the training figure. For computationally inexpensive models on small datasets this omission is negligible. For the \gls{mlp} on HIGGS it is not: as shown in \Cref{fig:tuning_costs}, a deliberately constrained 40-trial \gls{tpe} search required 72.3 hours and emitted 3{,}180\,\gls{gco2eq}, equivalent to approximately 27\,km of car travel \parencite{europeanenvironmentagencyMonitoringCO2Emissions2025} or roughly 418 full smartphone charges on the German electricity grid, a figure that exceeds the combined training emissions of all Wine and Credit runs in this study. This is not the outcome of an exhaustive search of the kind documented by \citeauthor{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019a} \parencite{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019a}, who found that large \gls{nlp} development pipelines required hundreds of thousands of \gls{gpu} hours to converge. It is the cost of the minimum credible search budget, chosen specifically to limit overhead.

How expensive that search becomes is structurally determined by how well a model's training procedure maps onto available hardware during each trial evaluation. On the Credit dataset, this difference is already quantifiable. \gls{xgb} completed its tuning study in 122 seconds and 1.92\,\gls{gco2eq}, accelerating each trial on \gls{gpu}. \gls{rf} arrived at a functionally identical result, a \gls{wf1} of 80.0\,\% against \gls{xgb}'s 80.2\,\%, in 326 seconds and 6.09\,\gls{gco2eq}: three times the tuning cost for a predictive difference that is statistically negligible. The gap reflects an architectural property that scales with search rather than just with training: constructing an ensemble of independent decision trees requires sequential or coarse-grained parallel computation that does not exploit \gls{gpu} cores. Every trial inherits that cost and forty trials compound it. This is why \gls{rf} was excluded from HIGGS tuning altogether, with literature defaults substituted instead. Subjecting that per-trial cost to eleven million rows across forty iterations would have been ecologically unjustifiable relative to any predictive gain it could have delivered, an assessment the scaling analysis in \Cref{sec:results_scaling} corroborates.

The consequence for how the benchmark results in the following sections should be interpreted is direct. The training emissions in the benchmark table represent the cost of a single run with already-optimized hyperparameters. For the HIGGS \gls{mlp}, the initialization cost that precedes that run amounts to 3{,}180\,\gls{gco2eq} against a training footprint of 58.5\,\gls{gco2eq}: tuning is roughly 54 times costlier than the benchmark run it makes possible. That debt does not reset at deployment. A nested \gls{cv} approach for hyperparameter evaluation, as described in \Cref{design_validation}, would have multiplied these costs fivefold, adding weeks of additional compute on HIGGS alone. Whether the \gls{mlp}'s efficient inference architecture can serve enough predictions to offset that initialization cost over a realistic deployment lifetime is a question the lifecycle analysis in \Cref{sec:results_lifecycle} addresses directly.

Translating tuning costs into the \gls{cf1} framework extends this picture beyond absolute emissions. \Cref{fig:tuning_cf1} reports the $\frac{\gls{mgco2eq}}{\gls{wf1}}$ achieved across each tuning study, using the best \gls{wf1} reached over 40 trials as the denominator. On Wine, all three models are tuned at a comparable efficiency of 6--12\,$\frac{\gls{mgco2eq}}{\gls{wf1}}$, with differences small enough to be practically irrelevant at this scale. On Credit, \gls{xgb} separates from the field: its tuning \gls{cf1} is three times lower than \gls{rf}'s and more than four times lower than \gls{mlp}'s (\Cref{tab:tuning_costs}). The gap reflects the same structural property that appears in training: \gls{xgb}'s histogram-based search is computationally light per trial, while tree-ensemble and gradient-descent-based searches carry proportionally higher per-trial costs. On HIGGS, the disparity becomes extreme. \gls{mlp}'s tuning \gls{cf1} is 57 times larger than \gls{xgb}'s, reflecting the combination of long per-trial evaluations on eleven million rows and a modest \gls{wf1} gain achieved over the full 40-trial budget. The tuning \gls{cf1} therefore reveals a cost axis that raw emissions alone obscure: whether the performance gains unlocked by a search budget are proportionate to its ecological price.

\Cref{tab:tuned_vs_default} makes the value proposition of hyperparameter tuning concrete by comparing the full initialization cost (HPO plus one training run) against simply training with framework defaults, restricting the comparison to HIGGS where the default configurations are defined as follows. For \gls{xgb}, the
library defaults are used directly: 100 estimators, maximum tree depth
of~6, and a learning rate of~0.3. For the \gls{mlp}, no authoritative
library default exists because the implementation uses a custom
\texttt{PyTorch} module rather than \texttt{scikit-learn}'s
\texttt{MLPClassifier}. A minimal baseline was therefore constructed to
approximate common framework conventions: Adam with a learning rate
of~0.001 and an early stopping patience of~10, both matching
\texttt{MLPClassifier}'s defaults, with two hidden layers of 100~units
each. The unit count follows \texttt{MLPClassifier}'s default of
\texttt{hidden\_layer\_sizes=(100,)}, and the depth is extended to two
layers as the smallest configuration that constitutes a genuinely
multi-layer network.

For both \gls{xgb} variants the HPO study absorbs more than 95\,\% of the total initialization budget: 54.3\,g out of a combined 57.1\,g for the \gls{cpu} variant. Against a default training cost of just 0.5\,g, the 1.9 percentage point \gls{wf1} gain from tuning carries an additional 56.6\,\gls{gco2eq}, roughly 30\,g per \gls{wf1} percentage point gained. For the \gls{mlp} the structural picture differs in one direction. Poorly matched defaults extend convergence, so the untuned model requires more epochs before early stopping triggers and produces a higher training cost (84.8\,g) than the tuned variant (58.5\,g). Better hyperparameters therefore directly reduce training energy as a side effect. Despite this training-side advantage, the HPO study itself costs 3{,}182.5\,g, 54 times the tuned run and 37 times the default run, making the total initialization budget 3{,}241\,g against 85\,g: a factor of 38 for a \gls{wf1} gain of 1.6 percentage points. In all three models the HPO study absorbs more than 95\,\% of the initialization budget, making the benchmark training figures a systematically small fraction of each model's true ecological cost to produce. Whether the performance premium justifies the cost is deployment-dependent: for a system serving millions of queries the additional \gls{wf1} points may translate into a meaningful outcome improvement; for a low-volume application competitive performance is available at a fraction of the price by accepting library defaults.

% ── Main Benchmark ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Main Cross-Dataset Benchmark}
\label{sec:results_main}

\expref{1} spans 14 model-dataset combinations across three datasets that differ by three orders of magnitude in size, directly addressing \rqref{1}. \Cref{fig:vergleich_performance,fig:vergleich_efficiency} lay out the two dimensions of the comparison. The \gls{cf1} ranking synthesizes both dimensions. The central finding is that no model dominates across all three settings. Ecological efficiency is not a fixed property of any single model. It emerges from the alignment between a model's computational profile, the hardware it runs on and the scale of the task it faces. Each of the three datasets exposes a different axis of that contingency. The emission figures in the per-dataset tables below reflect a single training run with already-optimized hyperparameters. A consolidated overview of all 14 model-dataset combinations is provided in \Cref{tab:main_results} in the Appendix. The one-time initialization cost of producing those parameters and the \gls{wf1} premium they deliver over untuned defaults are quantified in \Cref{sec:results_tuning}.

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{The Low-Data Regime: When Hardware Overhead Dominates.}
\label{sec:results_wine}

Focusing first on the Wine dataset, the leftmost cluster in \Cref{fig:vergleich_performance} reveals that all five models converge on a \gls{wf1} of 97.2\,\%. With predictive differentiation absent, the entire comparison reduces to emissions, where \Cref{fig:vergleich_efficiency} shows a threefold spread across models that deliver the exact same outcome. The most striking observation within that spread is that \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} emits 1.3 times as much \gls{gco2eq} as \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu} for an identical result.

\begin{figure}[htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{vergleich_performance_f1.pdf}
\caption[\acrshort{wf1} across datasets.]{\Gls{wf1} across all 14 model-dataset combinations. On Wine, all five models converge on a \gls{wf1} of 97.2\,\%, making emissions the only meaningful dimension of comparison. On HIGGS, \gls{lr} saturates well below the non-linear models and \gls{mlp} achieves the highest single result in the study.}
\label{fig:vergleich_performance}
\end{figure}


The mechanism behind this reversal is the fixed cost of \gls{gpu} initialization and data movement. On a dataset of 178 rows, a Wine training run completes in fractions of a second. Device initialization and host-to-device memory transfers via the PCIe interface consume a fixed block of time and energy regardless of how little data follows. Consequently, the thousands of parallel cores the \gls{gpu} provides remain almost entirely idle for the actual computation. The overhead dominates before the workload can even begin. This is the structural condition under which \gls{simt} parallelism becomes a liability rather than an advantage: when data volume is too small to saturate the available multiprocessors, the architecture's fixed entry costs cannot be amortized.

The \gls{cf1} ranking (\Cref{tab:wine_results}) surfaces this disproportionality directly. Since all five models achieve identical \gls{wf1}, the ranking collapses to emissions alone: \gls{lr} leads, while \gls{mlp} costs three times as much for the same outcome. Applying complex and power-hungry hardware to a problem that a linear model solves equally well is not merely suboptimal but ecologically wasteful.

\paragraph{The Medium-Data Regime: Structural Efficiency Disparities.}
\label{sec:results_credit}

Moving to the medium-scale Credit dataset (center cluster in \Cref{fig:vergleich_performance} and \Cref{fig:vergleich_efficiency}), genuine predictive differentiation appears for the first time. As \Cref{tab:credit_results} shows, the critical data point is \gls{rf}: it matches \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu}'s \gls{wf1} to one decimal place while incurring more than twice the emissions cost. Two models, statistically indistinguishable predictive outcomes and a more-than-twofold difference in ecological cost.

The explanation is structural rather than incidental. \gls{rf} constructs an ensemble of independently grown decision trees, evaluating candidate splits at each node across every tree in full. \gls{xgb}'s boosted tree construction, by contrast, uses highly-optimized histogram-based split-finding and grows trees sequentially, each correcting the residual errors of the last. This procedure achieves equivalent predictive capacity at a fraction of the computational cost. Crucially, this is the same structural property that inflated \gls{rf}'s tuning cost in \Cref{sec:results_tuning}: the per-tree overhead that makes tuning expensive makes training expensive for identical reasons and no amount of hyperparameter optimization removes it.

The \gls{cf1} column in \Cref{tab:credit_results} captures this disproportionality directly. \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu} leads despite near-identical \gls{wf1} to \gls{rf}; \gls{lr} ranks second even though it posts the lowest predictive score on this dataset, because its near-zero training cost outweighs the \gls{wf1} shortfall. \gls{mlp} finishes last: a 0.4 percentage point lead over \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu} comes at 3.5 times the training emissions. The Credit ranking therefore illustrates two complementary efficiency failure modes: the structural overhead of tree-ensemble computation and the disproportionate carbon cost of marginal neural-network gains. When model families differ structurally in computational profile, choosing the right family is at least as consequential for ecological efficiency as any hardware decision \parencite{verdecchiaDataCentricGreenAI2022a}.

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{The High-Data Regime: Hardware Saturation and Role Reversals.}
\label{sec:results_higgs}

Finally, as shown in the rightmost clusters of \Cref{fig:vergleich_performance} and \Cref{fig:vergleich_efficiency}, both dimensions of the comparison shift decisively at eleven million rows. \gls{rf} is excluded from this dataset for the reasons established in \Cref{sec:results_tuning}: the per-trial training cost made a full 40-trial HIGGS run ecologically unjustifiable. As \Cref{tab:higgs_results} shows, \gls{lr} saturates well below the non-linear models, while \gls{mlp} achieves the highest \gls{wf1} in the entire benchmark at the highest single-run training cost in the study. Most strikingly, \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} emits one seventh of what \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu} does for an equivalent outcome. On Wine, the \gls{gpu} was a liability. On HIGGS, it is the ecologically dominant choice.

The reversal follows directly from the \gls{simt} execution model discussed in \Cref{theoretical_background}. At the scale of eleven million rows, the data volume finally saturates the \gls{gpu}'s parallel cores. The fixed costs of device initialization and memory transfer that dominated the Wine result are now fully amortized across the dataset. \gls{xgb}'s histogram-based tree construction maps naturally onto this architecture, distributing split-finding workload across thousands of cores in a way that sequential \gls{cpu}-bound computation cannot match. \gls{lr}'s ceiling, by contrast, is independent of hardware: a linear decision boundary cannot partition the HIGGS feature space with sufficient fidelity regardless of the computational effort invested. The \gls{mlp}, meanwhile, requires iterative backpropagation passes over eleven million samples, driving its energy consumption to the highest single-run cost in the entire benchmark.

The \gls{cf1} column in \Cref{tab:higgs_results} inverts relative to raw \gls{wf1}. \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} leads by a wide margin while \gls{mlp}, despite holding the highest \gls{wf1} in the entire benchmark, finishes last. Its 2.5 percentage point predictive lead over \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} cannot compensate for the order-of-magnitude difference in training emissions at this scale. Whether deployment-scale inference changes this assessment is the subject of \Cref{sec:results_lifecycle}.

As established in the main benchmark, hardware acceleration is not unconditionally efficient. \Cref{fig:xgb_cpu_vs_gpu} isolates the performance of \gls{xgb} on \gls{cpu} and \gls{gpu} across all three datasets, revealing a contrast in ecological cost that shifts fundamentally with dataset scale.

On Wine and Credit, \gls{gpu} acceleration provides no emissions benefit but rather an ecological liability. The \gls{gpu} consistently emits more and trains slower than the \gls{cpu} on both small datasets. This inversion occurs because the fixed overhead of device initialization and host-to-device memory transfer via the PCIe interface completely dominates actual computation time, as analyzed in \Cref{sec:theoretical_cpu_vs_gpu}. The thousands of available cores remain starved of data, leaving the \gls{gpu}'s higher power draw unamortized. On Credit the gap narrows to near parity, but \gls{gpu} execution still fails to pay for itself.

The HIGGS result reverses the picture completely. The \gls{gpu} variant is seven times more emissions-efficient and nine times faster, as roughly nine million training rows finally saturate the \gls{gpu}'s \gls{simt} architecture \parencite{nickollsGPUComputingEra2010}. The histogram-based tree construction distributes efficiently across thousands of cores, and the fixed memory transfer costs that dominated at small scale are now fully amortized by the parallelization gains.

The analysis identifies a hard break-even threshold at approximately 80{,}000 rows. Below this volume, the \gls{gpu}'s higher instantaneous power draw remains unamortized, transforming the accelerator into an ecological penalty. Above it, the compounding speedup of massive parallelization outpaces the wattage premium and the emissions gap widens favorably with each additional row.

This finding provides a direct, empirical answer to \rqref{3}: the ecological viability of \gls{gpu} acceleration on tabular data is strictly bounded by dataset scale. More importantly, it challenges a pervasive assumption in modern machine learning. In practice, \gls{gpu} execution is frequently treated as a universal default: a switch flipped to ``accelerate'' computation regardless of the task. The evidence here demonstrates that for tabular data, this default is fundamentally flawed. Blindly applying a \gls{gpu} to a dataset of ten thousand rows does not accelerate learning. It merely burns energy to initialize a device and transfer data across a PCIe bus. True Green \gls{ai} requires practitioners to actively break this habit and align their hardware choices with the complexity of their workloads, treating accelerators not as an unconditional default, but as a specialized tool justified only by sufficient scale.

\section{HIGGS Dataset Scaling}
\label{sec:results_scaling}

\expref{2}, directly addressing \rqref{4}, tracks emissions and predictive performance across nine HIGGS subsets ranging from 1{,}000 to 8{,}800{,}000 rows (\Cref{fig:scaling_higgs}). Each data point is the mean across three independent random seeds. This replication averages out subsample-specific noise and yields monotonically well-behaved scaling curves. By holding the dataset complexity constant and isolating the volume of data, the scaling analysis exposes the exact thresholds at which the ecological cost of processing more data eclipses the predictive benefit.

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{Capacity Saturation in Simple Models}
At the lower end of the scaling curve, the linear boundary of \gls{lr} hits a hard capacity ceiling remarkably early. The model's \gls{wf1} improves from 62.0\,\% at 1{,}000 rows to 63.7\,\% at 100{,}000 rows, but remains essentially flat across all subsequent, larger subsets. Because the model lacks the representational capacity to capture the non-linear intricacies of the HIGGS dataset, additional data yields virtually no predictive return. Consequently, any compute expended to train this model beyond 100{,}000 rows is ecologically wasted, demonstrating that data volume must always be matched to model capacity.

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{The Architectural Bottleneck of Ensembles}
As the dataset scales to 500{,}000 rows, a striking reversal in the expected cost of model complexity emerges. \gls{rf}, despite its conceptual simplicity relative to deep learning, becomes the most ecologically expensive model in the study. At this scale, \gls{rf} emits 1.88\,\gls{gco2eq} to achieve a \gls{wf1} of 73.2\,\%. In stark contrast, the \gls{mlp} achieves a higher \gls{wf1} of 75.3\,\% while emitting just over half the \gls{co2eq} (0.99\,g) and \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu} achieves 74.0\,\% for just 0.11\,g. \gls{rf} is ultimately excluded from runs above 500{,}000 rows due to prohibitive training times. The underlying reason is architectural: training an ensemble of independent, fully-grown decision trees relies on coarse-grained parallelism that scales super-linearly with data volume and cannot easily exploit \gls{gpu} acceleration (see \Cref{par:rf_scalability}). This proves that a model's ecological footprint is not determined by its abstract mathematical complexity, but by how efficiently its training procedure maps onto available hardware parallelism.

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{Diminishing Returns in the High-Data Regime}
In the high-data regime above 1{,}000{,}000 rows, all non-linear models face the fundamental tension of empirical learning curves (introduced in \Cref{sec:theoretical_learning_curves}): predictive performance scales sub-linearly, while energy consumption scales linearly or worse. The observed \gls{wf1} curves across all models are qualitatively consistent with the concave power-law shape $P(n) \propto n^\alpha$ described there \parencite{koshuteRecommendingTrainingSet2021}: gains are steep at small subset sizes and flatten progressively as the dataset approaches its full scale. The \gls{mlp} illustrates this trade-off most dramatically. Expanding its training data from 1 million to 8.8 million rows yields the largest absolute performance gain in this regime, improving the \gls{wf1} from 76.3\,\% to 78.6\,\%. However, this 2.2 percentage point improvement requires a 29-fold increase in emissions. Framed as a stopping decision, a practitioner who caps \gls{mlp} training at one million rows instead of 8.8 million reduces emissions by 97\,\% while accepting a 2.9\,\% relative loss in \gls{wf1}: a compelling case for treating dataset size as a tunable budget rather than a fixed input. Conversely, \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} navigates this regime with extreme efficiency, reaching a \gls{wf1} of 76.0\,\% at 8.8 million rows while emitting only 0.37\,\gls{gco2eq}, roughly one sixth of its \gls{cpu} counterpart (2.32\,g).

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{Synthesis: Rethinking the Big Data Dogma}
Assessed through the \gls{cf1} lens, the diverse scaling patterns compress into a single, clarifying trend: as dataset size grows, the emissions gap between hardware-aligned models and inefficient models drastically widens. \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} sustains the lowest \gls{cf1} across every scale above its hardware break-even point, proving that large-scale learning is only ecologically viable when software is perfectly aligned with \gls{simt} acceleration. At five million rows it achieves a \gls{cf1} of 3.04 against \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu}'s 17.25 at the same \gls{wf1} of 75.8\,\%, a gap that widens further at the full 8.8 million row dataset.

Grey cells indicate runs not performed (\gls{rf} excluded above 500{,}000 rows).}

\end{figure}

More profoundly, these scaling curves challenge the pervasive assumption that more data is unconditionally better, which drives modern machine learning. In the pursuit of state-of-the-art performance, the default industrial practice is to feed models the largest dataset available \parencite{schwartzGreenAI2020}. The empirical evidence here proves that this practice is often an ecological liability. At the tail end of a learning curve, forcing a model to ingest ten million additional rows to extract a marginal gain in \gls{wf1} results in a disproportionate explosion of carbon emissions. True Green \gls{ai} requires practitioners to abandon the assumption that all available data must be used. Instead, as recent data-centric \gls{ai} studies argue, we must proactively identify the threshold where predictive returns diminish \parencite{verdecchiaDataCentricGreenAI2022a}, treating dataset size not as a fixed asset, but as a hyperparameter that must be aggressively tuned to halt training before the ecological cost eclipses the scientific value.

The variation grid reveals that width has a substantially stronger effect on performance than depth. Moving from 128 to 512 units per layer increases \gls{wf1} by up to 2.7 percentage points across all depth levels, whereas adding layers beyond two yields marginal gains of at most 0.7 percentage points at the widest setting and near zero for narrower networks. The predictive ceiling is reached early: the 2$\times$512 architecture achieves a \gls{wf1} of 77.6\,\%, trailing the best-observed configuration (4$\times$512, \gls{wf1}\,=\,78.3\,\%) by only 0.7 percentage points.

The emissions picture is more nuanced. For narrow networks (width\,=\,128), emissions actually \textit{decrease} as the network gets deeper, falling from 26\,\gls{gco2eq} at depth~1 to 18\,\gls{gco2eq} at depth~3. This reversal is mechanically driven by early stopping. Deep, narrow networks lack the representational capacity required for complex tabular features, causing them to rapidly overfit and trigger the early stopping patience criterion in fewer epochs. Consequently, the total compute per training run is lower despite the larger parameter count. For wider networks, this effect vanishes. At width\,=\,512, emissions increase monotonically with depth from 48\,\gls{gco2eq} to 93\,\gls{gco2eq}, as the network successfully utilizes its capacity and trains for more epochs before saturating.

Taken together, these results highlight a structural mismatch between standard deep learning practice and tabular data. In computer vision or natural language processing, stacking layers is necessary to extract hierarchical spatial or sequential representations. Tabular datasets like HIGGS lack this strict local hierarchy, so excessive depth provides almost no predictive value and functions primarily as a computational overhead. The 4$\times$512 configuration illustrates this directly: achieving the final 0.7 percentage point improvement in \gls{wf1} required scaling to maximum depth and width, nearly doubling the emission footprint from 55\,\gls{gco2eq} to 93\,\gls{gco2eq} for a gain well within normal run-to-run variance. Framed as an architecture selection decision, choosing 2$\times$512 over 4$\times$512 reduces emissions by 41\,\% while sacrificing less than one percentage point of \gls{wf1} in relative terms. The data here corroborate the broader Green \gls{ai} observation that predictive returns on model scale follow diminishing returns, while energy costs do not \parencite{schwartzGreenAI2020}.

Evaluating the twelve configurations through the \gls{cf1} lens, as seen in \Cref{fig:mlp_variation_cf1} makes this trade-off explicit. Shallower, narrower architectures achieve lower \gls{cf1} scores: their modest performance reductions are more than offset by their lower training costs. For neural networks applied to tabular data, this suggests treating depth not as a default but as a resource justified only by proportional predictive returns.

\section{Carbon Intensity Analysis}
\label{sec:results_carbon}

The analyses conducted in the preceding sections treat emissions as a fixed function of the training run: given the same model, dataset and hardware, the reported figure does not change. This is a consequence of how CodeCarbon works: it converts measured energy into \gls{gco2eq} using a static intensity factor of $381\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$ for Germany, a calculated average that cannot reflect when a run actually takes place (see \Cref{sec:theoretical_codecarbon}). The German grid, however, draws on a fundamentally different fuel mix in January than in July and during peak evening demand than during solar-rich midday hours, a structure visible directly in the hourly ElectricityMaps data used in this analysis.

    \caption{January 2025 (21--30 Jan). Gas, coal and nuclear dominate, with solar generation near zero.}
    \label{fig:electricity_mix_jan}
\end{subfigure}

\vspace{0.8em}

\begin{subfigure}[t]{0.9\linewidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{electricity_mix_germany_july.pdf}
    \caption{July 2025 (1--10 Jul). Solar becomes the dominant midday source, sharply reducing the fossil fuel share during daylight hours.}
    \label{fig:electricity_mix_jul}
\end{subfigure}
\caption[Electricity mix in Germany: winter vs.\ summer.]{Electricity production mix in Germany for representative ten-day windows in winter and summer. \label{fig:electricity_mix_comparison}

\Cref{fig:electricity_mix_jan,fig:electricity_mix_jul} illustrate this contrast directly. In January, dispatchable sources (gas, coal and nuclear) form the base of the stack, with wind providing a variable but significant contribution and solar generation remaining negligible throughout the day. In July, the picture reverses: solar becomes the dominant midday source, regularly exceeding 20{,}000\,MW, while gas and coal drop to a much smaller share. It is this structural shift in fuel composition that drives the seasonal variation in carbon intensity quantified in the analysis below.

If this temporal variation is large enough, then the timing of a training run is itself a meaningful ecological decision, one that requires no changes to code, hardware, or model architecture. This section presents the results of \expref{4}, which evaluates that hypothesis by counterfactually re-assigning the energy of every training run to different timing scenarios derived from one year of hourly ElectricityMaps data, as described in \Cref{design_carbon_intensity}.

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{Annual Spread and Scenario Intensities}
The first result is somewhat reassuring for the existing measurements: as shown in \Cref{fig:scenario_intensities}, the static CodeCarbon factor of $381\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$ is a defensible approximation of the annual mean of the year 2024, which the empirical data places at $341\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$. A workload executed at a uniformly random point during the year would therefore be assigned only a modest systematic error by CodeCarbon. The more important finding is the spread around that average. The best recorded hour of 2024 reaches $115\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$ and the worst reaches $658\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$, a factor of 5.7$\times$ between two hours. The absolute extremes are not practically exploitable, since no practitioner can guarantee their run coincides with the single cleanest or dirtiest hour of the year. The structurally informed seasonal-diurnal combinations are the actionable range: summer midday hours average $193\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$ while winter evening hours average $389\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$, a factor of two achievable through deliberate scheduling alone.

\paragraph{Per-Run Emissions Counterfactuals}
Because training energy is a fixed physical quantity for any given run, every percentage difference in grid intensity translates directly into the same percentage difference in emissions. \Cref{fig:scenario_per_run} expresses this proportionality relative to the static CodeCarbon baseline, making the stakes concrete. Scheduling during summer mean conditions would reduce the reported emissions of this study by approximately 20\,\% compared to the static estimate, requiring no modification to the experimental setup whatsoever. The best-hour scenario pushes this reduction to 70\,\%, while the worst-hour scenario reverses it into a 73\,\% increase. These percentages are identical for every model and dataset, because the intensity factor scales uniformly. What differs is the absolute magnitude of the effect. For lightweight runs such as \gls{lr} on Wine, the difference between any two scenarios is negligible in absolute terms. For the \gls{mlp} trained on HIGGS, the gap between best-hour and worst-hour timing amounts to approximately 208\,\gls{gco2eq}, a quantity that exceeds the total training footprint of every Wine and Credit run in this study combined. This asymmetry reveals an important practical principle: grid-aware scheduling is most valuable precisely where model and hardware efficiency decisions also matter most, namely on large, compute-intensive workloads where the absolute energy expenditure is high enough for timing to make a meaningful difference.

During the core daylight hours, the static factor overestimates the actual intensity by up to a factor of two. This discrepancy empirically validates recent arguments that relying on generation-based average rates is inadequate for true consequential carbon accounting, which instead requires time-specific marginal emissions data \parencite{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022}. This is not an anomaly specific to this particular date: it is the structural consequence of Germany's growing solar capacity, which consistently depresses midday intensity throughout the summer months. A practitioner who runs training jobs during office hours on summer days is inadvertently benefiting from this effect, while their CodeCarbon reports attribute a higher carbon cost than actually occurred.

\paragraph{Predictable Structure and Actionable Scheduling}
The year-long heatmap in \Cref{fig:diurnal_seasonal} brings both dimensions together and makes their joint structure immediately legible. The lowest-intensity region of the entire year is summer midday: a consistent band of green running from May through August between approximately 10:00 and 15:00, reflecting the hours when solar generation is both abundant and at its seasonal peak. The highest-intensity region is winter evenings: the band of red and orange concentrated between 17:00 and 21:00 from November through February, when solar output has already dropped to near zero and space heating drives gas and coal generation. What the heatmap makes clear is that these two extremes are not separated by chance. They are structurally determined by the interaction of Germany's solar build-out with the seasonal daylight cycle and heating demand patterns. A practitioner optimizing scheduling on local infrastructure is therefore not searching for a random low-intensity moment. They are navigating a predictable structure where the best and worst times are largely known in advance. The summer-midday to winter-evening gradient spans the full two-to-one ratio identified earlier and represents the maximum emissions reduction achievable through scheduling on the German grid without any hardware or model changes. Exploiting these predictable patterns through elastic, carbon-aware workload scheduling thus emerges as a primary strategy for decarbonizing machine learning \parencite{wuSustainableAIEnvironmental2022, dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022}.

\paragraph{Synthesis}
% LTeX: enabled=false
Taken together, these findings introduce a dimension of ecological efficiency that the main benchmark cannot capture: timing. The static CodeCarbon factor is not grossly wrong as an annual average, but it flattens a distribution that is wide enough to change the practical interpretation of the results. It is worth noting that this intensity overestimation of roughly 12\,\% (381 versus $341\,\frac{\gls{gco2eq}}{\gls{kwh}}$) acts in the \textit{opposite} direction to the \gls{tdp}-based underestimation documented in \Cref{sec:results_measurement}, which inflates the corrected value by a median factor of 1.87. The intensity error is small enough that the energy-measurement error dominates in every case. The net effect of uncorrected CodeCarbon is still substantial undercounting. The emissions reported in this study should therefore be understood as contingent on when the runs were executed, not as intrinsic properties of the models. For small datasets such as Wine and Credit, this contingency is inconsequential in absolute terms, as the runs are too cheap for timing to shift the total by a meaningful amount. For large-scale workloads such as the \gls{mlp} on HIGGS, grid-aware scheduling would have reduced the training footprint by the equivalent of the entire Credit dataset's combined model training budget. At that scale, scheduling is no longer a marginal consideration but a first-order efficiency lever, comparable in impact to moderate algorithmic improvements and achievable without touching a single line of model code.

The lowest break-even point is \gls{rf} on Wine, at 66 predictions. \gls{rf} inference is slow relative to other classifiers: each prediction requires sequential traversal of the learned tree ensemble, producing a per-prediction cost of approximately 236\,\textmu\gls{gco2eq}. Against a training footprint of only 0.016\,\gls{gco2eq}, the inference cost accumulates to parity within a few minutes of active serving. For any realistic \gls{rf} deployment on tabular data at this scale, inference is the dominant ecological cost by a wide margin.

The opposite extreme is \gls{mlp} on HIGGS, which does not reach break-even until approximately 8.3 million predictions. \gls{mlp} inference is fast: the forward pass through a fixed-depth network requires no tree traversal, producing a per-prediction cost of only 7.1\,\textmu\gls{gco2eq}. Against a training footprint of 58.5\,\gls{gco2eq}, that rate of accumulation reaches parity only after roughly 8 million queries. A system serving this model on a low-traffic application would find that training represents the dominant ecological cost throughout its entire operational lifetime.

Between these extremes, break-even points organize around two drivers: training scale and inference latency. \gls{lr} on Credit breaks even at 5{,}551 predictions because both costs are modest in absolute terms. \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} on HIGGS breaks even at 9{,}643 predictions: its training footprint is contained at 0.39\,\gls{gco2eq}, but inference at 40.7\,\textmu\gls{gco2eq} per prediction accumulates faster than \gls{mlp}'s 7.1\,\textmu\gls{gco2eq}, so the budget is recovered at a far lower deployment volume. The contrast between \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} (9{,}643) and \gls{mlp} (8.3 million) on the same dataset illustrates a deeper trade-off in model design. \gls{mlp}'s fast inference pushes the break-even point to a scale that most low-traffic applications will never reach, concentrating the ecological cost at training time. \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu}'s slower inference distributes the lifecycle cost more evenly across the deployment period.

\paragraph{Cross-Model Lifecycle Comparison on HIGGS}

\begin{figure}[htb]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{lifecycle_xgb_gpu_vs_mlp_higgs.pdf}
\caption[Cumulative lifecycle \acrshort{co2eq} on HIGGS as a function of prediction volume.]{Cumulative lifecycle emissions for all four HIGGS models (\gls{lr}, \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu}, \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu}, \gls{mlp}) as a function of prediction volume. Models with cheap training but slow inference (\gls{xgb} \gls{gpu}, \gls{xgb} \gls{cpu}) accumulate cost faster than models with expensive training but fast inference (\gls{lr}, \gls{mlp}).}
\label{fig:lifecycle_xgb_gpu_vs_mlp_higgs}
\end{figure}

The individual break-even points quantify when each model's inference cost overtakes its own training investment. A complementary and more deployment-relevant question is which of two competing models accumulates less total lifecycle emissions at a given query volume. \Cref{fig:lifecycle_xgb_gpu_vs_mlp_higgs} addresses this directly for \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} and \gls{mlp} on HIGGS, the two models that dominate the HIGGS result in opposite directions of the efficiency-performance trade-off.

\gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} enters deployment with a training footprint of 0.39\,\gls{gco2eq}. \gls{mlp} carries a training debt of 58.5\,\gls{gco2eq}, a factor of 149 larger. The two cumulative cost curves begin from these starting points and converge because inference accumulates at different rates: 40.7\,\textmu\gls{gco2eq} per prediction for \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} against 7.1\,\textmu\gls{gco2eq} for \gls{mlp}, a factor of 5.8 in the opposite direction. The cross-over occurs at approximately 1.73 million predictions, the query volume at which \gls{mlp}'s lower per-prediction cost has fully amortized its much larger training investment. Beyond that threshold, \gls{mlp} is the more ecologically efficient of the two for every additional prediction served.

Whether that threshold is reached depends entirely on deployment context. A batch scoring job processing ten million records in a single run would cross the threshold within that run and favor \gls{mlp} on lifecycle grounds despite its 149-fold higher training cost. A low-traffic internal tool serving a few thousand queries per month would never approach the threshold, leaving \gls{xgb} \gls{gpu} as the lifecycle-optimal choice throughout its operational lifetime. The cross-over point therefore acts as a deployment-scale decision boundary that is orthogonal to predictive performance and invisible in any training-only benchmark table.

The roughly five orders of magnitude separating these extremes reflect a structural divide rather than an accident of scale. Models with cheap training and slow inference front-load their ecological cost at the point of training, then spend that cost again with every subsequent prediction. Models with expensive training and fast inference invert this: the initial investment is large, but it is amortized across many cheap queries. Which profile is ecologically preferable depends entirely on how many predictions the deployed system will serve, a figure that has nothing to do with performance or training efficiency \parencite{luccioniPowerHungryProcessing2024}.

This creates a direct inversion of the training-only rankings for high-volume deployments \parencite{desislavovTrendsAIInference2023}. \gls{rf} on Wine achieves a favorable \gls{cf1} precisely because it is cheap to train. Yet at ten thousand predictions it has already accumulated roughly 95 times its training budget in inference emissions, while \gls{mlp} at the same query count has barely touched its much larger training investment. For a practitioner deploying at scale, the \gls{cf1} ranking is not merely incomplete at this point. It points in the wrong direction. Ecological model selection therefore requires a deployment volume estimate as an explicit input. Below the break-even threshold, optimizing training emissions is the right lever. Above it, inference latency per prediction governs the total lifecycle cost and no amount of training efficiency compensates for a slow prediction function at scale.
