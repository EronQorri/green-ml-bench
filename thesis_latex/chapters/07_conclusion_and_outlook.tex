\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Single hardware platform and grid.}
    All measurements are specific to one machine (AMD Ryzen~7 5700X, RTX~4070) and the
    German electricity grid. Absolute emission and energy figures do not generalize
    directly to other hardware configurations or geographic regions. Relative efficiency
    rankings are more transferable, as they reflect algorithmic properties rather than
    hardware specifics alone.

    \item \textbf{Small Wine test set.}
    An 80/20 split of the 178-instance Wine dataset yields approximately 36 test samples,
    producing a noisier performance estimate than the thousands or millions of test
    instances available for Credit and HIGGS. This is a structural constraint of the
    dataset size rather than a methodological choice.

    \item \textbf{Fixed 40-trial \gls{hpo} budget.}
    The uniform 40-trial Optuna budget does not account for differences in search space
    dimensionality across models. The \gls{mlp} search space is substantially larger and
    structurally more complex than those of Random Forest or XGBoost; reported \gls{mlp}
    performance figures should therefore be interpreted as a lower bound on what a more
    exhaustive search might achieve.

    \item \textbf{Random Forest excluded from the full HIGGS benchmark.}
    Prohibitive training runtimes at 11 million instances prevented Random Forest from
    being included in the main cross-dataset benchmark and in the scaling experiment above
    500{,}000 rows, leaving the cross-model comparison at large scale incomplete for this
    algorithm family.

    \item \textbf{Random Forest not tuned on HIGGS.}
    In the dataset scaling experiment, hyperparameters for Random Forest were taken from
    literature defaults rather than Optuna tuning, because the per-trial cost at large
    subset sizes was deemed ecologically unjustifiable. Performance figures for Random
    Forest at larger HIGGS subsets may therefore be suboptimal.

    \item \textbf{Hyperparameters over-specified for small subsets in the scaling
    experiment.}
    The hyperparameters used across all subset sizes were tuned on the full
    11-million-instance HIGGS dataset and were not re-optimised per subset. At small
    subsets the model is configured for a much larger dataset; reported accuracy figures
    at 1{,}000 or 10{,}000 instances are therefore lower bounds on what a properly tuned
    model could achieve at those scales.

    \item \textbf{\gls{cf1} is grid-dependent and absolute values are not directly
    comparable across datasets.}
    CO\textsubscript{2}eq emissions, and therefore \gls{cf1}, are tied to the carbon
    intensity of the local electricity grid. Additionally, direct comparison of absolute
    \gls{cf1} values across datasets is limited because task difficulty, and thus
    inherent compute demand, varies across Wine, Credit and HIGGS.

    \item \textbf{Static carbon intensity factor.}
    CodeCarbon converts measured energy to CO\textsubscript{2} using a fixed annual
    average of 381\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh} for Germany, which cannot reflect diurnal or
    seasonal variation in grid composition. All reported CO\textsubscript{2} figures
    therefore carry an implicit timing uncertainty; the temporal carbon intensity analysis
    in \Cref{sec:results_carbon} bounds this uncertainty but cannot eliminate it from the
    primary measurements.

    \item \textbf{\gls{cpu} power measurement is Windows-specific and requires elevated
    privileges.}
    The hardware-corrected emissions rely on LibreHardwareMonitor, a Windows-only
    solution. All measurements were collected under administrator rights; without elevated
    privileges the \texttt{CPUPowerMonitor} cannot access hardware sensors and the
    corrected CO\textsubscript{2} estimate cannot be computed. The corrected values
    reported in this study cannot be reproduced directly on Linux or macOS.

    \item \textbf{Background system activity.}
    CodeCarbon measures at the process level, meaning Windows~11 background processes
    contribute to the recorded energy. This effect is expected to be small relative to the
    dominant training workload but cannot be fully isolated or quantified.

    \item \textbf{\gls{cpu} parallelization asymmetry and absence of an idle baseline.}
    Logistic Regression uses a single-threaded solver and runs at approximately 10\,\%
    \gls{cpu} utilisation, while Random Forest and XGBoost saturate all eight cores via
    \texttt{n\_jobs=-1}. At 10\,\% load, the idle component of \gls{cpu} Package Power
    represents a disproportionately large share of the measured wattage, meaning Logistic
    Regression's reported emissions likely overstate its true algorithmic energy cost
    relative to the multi-threaded models. No idle-power baseline was captured to correct
    for this effect.

    \item \textbf{\gls{gpu} idle draw not tracked during inference.}
    The per-prediction CO\textsubscript{2} estimate is derived from \gls{cpu} power
    measured directly during the inference timing loop. \gls{gpu} idle draw during
    inference is not tracked, so inference cost figures for \gls{gpu}-trained models
    (XGBoost \gls{gpu} and \gls{mlp}) represent a lower bound on the true per-prediction
    footprint.

\end{itemize}
