Across 317 CodeCarbon-tracked runs conducted between March and May 2026, the experiments in this study consumed approximately 10.7\,\gls{kwh} of electricity and 151.7 hours of compute time, with CodeCarbon logging a cumulative 4.06\,\gls{kgco2eq}, equivalent to driving approximately
35\,km in an average German car \parencite{europeanenvironmentagencyMonitoringCO2Emissions2025} or charging
a smartphone roughly 535 times on the German grid. Given the systematic underestimation documented in \Cref{sec:results_measurement}, the corrected total is substantially higher. The 4.06\,kg should be read as a lower bound. Hyperparameter tuning for the \gls{mlp} on HIGGS alone accounts for 2.47\,kg of that figure, more than 60\,\% of the logged total, illustrating that the experimental overhead of finding a competitive configuration can dwarf the cost of the benchmark runs themselves.

The chapter is organized in eight sections that together address the five sub-questions set out in \Cref{sec:research_questions}. \Cref{sec:results_measurement} first establishes the hardware-corrected measurement baseline on which all subsequent comparisons rest; \Cref{sec:results_tuning} then quantifies the tuning overhead that standard benchmark tables omit. The main analysis proceeds in four thematic blocks: the cross-dataset benchmark (\Cref{sec:results_main}), the \gls{cpu}--\gls{gpu} hardware comparison (\Cref{sec:results_xgb}), the HIGGS scaling and \gls{mlp} architecture variation experiments (\Cref{sec:results_scaling,sec:results_mlp_arch}) and finally the temporal and lifecycle analyses (\Cref{sec:results_carbon,sec:results_lifecycle}) that extend the efficiency picture beyond training time alone.

\section{Emissions Measurement Validation}
\label{sec:results_measurement}

Any comparison of model efficiencies is only as valid as the measurements it rests on. As described in \Cref{implementation_emissions}, CodeCarbon's \gls{tdp}-based \gls{cpu} estimate was replaced in this study by direct hardware-sensor readings. \Cref{fig:codecarbon_vs_hw} shows the ratio between the two for every model-dataset combination.

\label{fig:codecarbon_vs_hw}

Every one of the 14 model-dataset combinations shows a hardware-corrected value that
exceeds the raw CodeCarbon figure. The ratio ranges from 1.32 to 2.63 with a median of
1.87, meaning CodeCarbon, as deployed without modification, would have captured on average
only about 54\,\% of the true emissions value.
\citeauthor{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025}
\parencite{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} found a consistent underestimation of 20 to
30\% when validating CodeCarbon against external power meters across a range of workloads.
% TODO: update LR Wine ratio and percentage after rerun
The \gls{cpu}-intensive runs in this study exceed even that range. Logistic Regression on
Wine reaches a ratio of 2.62, meaning CodeCarbon captured less than 40\% of the actual
energy. The cause is the \gls{tdp} fallback described in \Cref{implementation_emissions}.
Where \gls{rapl} is unavailable, CodeCarbon estimates \gls{cpu} power from the thermal
design envelope rather than measuring it and that envelope does not track how the chip
actually behaves under load \parencite{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020}.

The error is not uniformly distributed and two structural factors account for most of the
variation. % TODO: update training time ranges, mean ratios, GPU/CPU energy shares and lowest ratio after rerun
Short runs are hit hardest. Wine combinations, where training times range from
0.04 to 3.35 seconds, show a mean ratio of 2.27, while HIGGS combinations (90 to
11{,}175 seconds) land at 1.55. On a sub-second run the \gls{tdp}-based integrator barely
accumulates anything while the chip executes a real burst of work. As duration grows and
load sustains, the heuristic becomes a closer approximation and the ratio shrinks. The
\gls{gpu} share is the second factor. XGBoost \gls{gpu} on HIGGS, where the \gls{gpu}
accounts for 58\,\% of total energy and the \gls{cpu} for only 31\,\%, produces the
lowest ratio at 1.32. Since the correction touches only the \gls{cpu} slice, a smaller
\gls{cpu} fraction produces a smaller total distortion even when the underlying power
discrepancy is comparable to other runs. \citeauthor{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025}
\parencite{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} document the same asymmetry, finding that
\gls{cpu}-only workloads showed errors exceeding \gls{gpu} equivalents by more than an
order of magnitude in some configurations.

% TODO: update LR Wine and XGBoost CPU Wine ratios after rerun
The measurement error is largest for the runs that correspond to the algorithms Green
\gls{ai} positions as the efficient choice. Logistic Regression on Wine carries a ratio
of 2.62, XGBoost \gls{cpu} on Wine 2.63. These are the models a practitioner would reach
for to minimize ecological footprint and they are precisely the ones where \gls{tdp}-based
estimation fails most severely. Without correction, they would appear even more
energy-efficient relative to the heavier workloads than the corrected figures show, because
the measurement error and the efficiency claim point in the same direction. The efficiency
advantage of lightweight models is confirmed by the corrected data, but the gap is smaller
than uncorrected measurements would suggest. All emissions values in the remainder of this
chapter use the hardware-corrected figures. The relative rankings are unchanged, but the
absolute scale is accurate. Published studies relying on uncorrected CodeCarbon values on
comparable hardware should be read as reporting lower bounds rather than ground truth.

\section{Hyperparameter Tuning Overhead}
\label{sec:results_tuning}

Benchmark tables that compare training emissions report only a fraction of a
model's true ecological initialization cost. Before a single benchmark run is
executed, each model must be brought to a competitive configuration through
hyperparameter search and the footprint of that search is never reflected in
the training figure. For computationally inexpensive models on small datasets
this omission is negligible. For the \gls{mlp} on HIGGS it is not: as shown
in \Cref{fig:tuning_costs}, % TODO: update MLP HIGGS tuning hours, CO2, car/phone analogies and comparison to Wine+Credit total after rerun
a deliberately constrained 40-trial \gls{tpe}
search required 95.4 hours and emitted 2.15\,kg of CO\textsubscript{2},
equivalent to approximately 18\,km of car travel
\parencite{europeanenvironmentagencyMonitoringCO2Emissions2025} or roughly
283 full smartphone charges on the German grid, a figure that exceeds the combined training emissions of all Wine and
Credit runs in this study. This is not the outcome of an exhaustive search of the kind
documented by \citeauthor{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019}
\parencite{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019}, who found that large
\gls{nlp} development pipelines required hundreds of thousands of \gls{gpu}
hours to converge. It is the cost of the minimum credible search budget,
chosen specifically to limit overhead.

How expensive that search becomes is structurally determined by how well an
algorithm's training procedure maps onto available hardware during each trial
evaluation. On the Credit dataset, this difference is already quantifiable.
% TODO: update XGBoost and RF tuning durations, CO2 and WF1 values on Credit after rerun
XGBoost completed its tuning study in 130 seconds and 1.08\,g
CO\textsubscript{2}, accelerating each trial on \gls{gpu}. Random Forest
arrived at a functionally identical result, a \gls{wf1} of 0.800 against
XGBoost's 0.801, in 815 seconds and 10.5\,g CO\textsubscript{2}: ten times
the tuning cost for a predictive difference that is statistically negligible.
The gap reflects an architectural property that scales with search rather than
just with training: constructing an ensemble of independent decision trees
requires sequential or coarse-grained parallel computation that does not
exploit \gls{gpu} cores. Every trial inherits that cost and forty trials
compound it. This is why Random Forest was excluded from HIGGS tuning
altogether, with literature defaults substituted instead. Subjecting that
per-trial cost to eleven million rows across forty iterations would have been
ecologically unjustifiable relative to any predictive gain it could have
delivered, an assessment the scaling analysis in
\Cref{sec:results_scaling} corroborates.

The consequence for how the benchmark results in the following sections should
be interpreted is direct. The training emissions in the benchmark table
represent the cost of a single run with already-optimized hyperparameters.
% TODO: update MLP HIGGS tuning CO2, training footprint and multiplier ratio after rerun
For the HIGGS \gls{mlp}, the initialization cost that precedes that run
amounts to 2.15\,kg against a training footprint of 145.9\,g: tuning is
roughly 15 times costlier than the benchmark run it makes possible. That debt does not reset at deployment. A nested \gls{cv}
approach for hyperparameter evaluation, as described in \Cref{design_validation}, would
have multiplied these costs fivefold, adding weeks of additional compute on
HIGGS alone. The 40-trial single-loop \todo{nicht mehr single-loop} design is the result of an active
ecological trade-off and the 2.15\,kg figure is the lower bound of what
responsible tuning entails at this scale. Whether the \gls{mlp}'s efficient
inference architecture can serve enough predictions to offset that
initialization cost over a realistic deployment lifetime is a question the
lifecycle analysis in \Cref{sec:results_lifecycle} addresses directly.

\paragraph{The Low-Data Regime: When Hardware Overhead Dominates}
\label{sec:results_wine}

Focusing first on the low-data regime, the leftmost cluster in
\Cref{fig:vergleich_performance} reveals that four of the five models reach
a \gls{wf1} of 0.989 on the Wine dataset. Random Forest at 0.983 is the
only outlier and the margin carries no practical significance. With
predictive differentiation absent, the entire comparison reduces to

across models that deliver the exact same outcome. The most striking
observation within that spread is that XGBoost \gls{gpu} emits 2.5 times
as much CO\textsubscript{2} as XGBoost \gls{cpu} for an identical result.

The mechanism behind this reversal is the fixed cost of \gls{gpu}
initialization and data movement. On a dataset of 178 rows, a Wine training
run completes in fractions of a second. Device initialization and
host-to-device memory transfers via the PCIe interface consume a fixed block
of time and energy regardless of how little data follows. Consequently, the
thousands of parallel cores the \gls{gpu} provides remain almost entirely
idle for the actual computation. The overhead dominates before the workload
can even begin. This is the structural condition under which \gls{simt}
parallelism becomes a liability rather than an advantage: when data volume
is too small to saturate the available multiprocessors, the architecture's
fixed entry costs cannot be amortized.

% TODO: update Wine CF1 ranking values after rerun
The \gls{cf1} metric is designed to surface exactly this kind of
disproportionality. Logistic Regression, which requires no \gls{gpu} and
imposes the lowest computational overhead, leads the \gls{cf1} ranking with the lowest score at
0.139\,mg/F1\%. This result perfectly illustrates a core principle of Green \gls{ai}: applying complex and power-hungry hardware
to a problem that a linear model solves equally well is not merely suboptimal
but ecologically wasteful.

\paragraph{The Medium-Data Regime: Algorithmic Efficiency Disparities}
\label{sec:results_credit}

Moving to the medium-scale Credit dataset (center cluster in
\Cref{fig:vergleich_performance} and \Cref{fig:vergleich_efficiency}),
genuine predictive differentiation appears for the first time.
\Gls{mlp} achieves the highest \gls{wf1} at 0.804 and XGBoost \gls{cpu}
reaches 0.800 at a training cost of 43.3\,mg CO\textsubscript{2}eq. The
critical data point, however, is Random Forest. It matches XGBoost
\gls{cpu}'s \gls{wf1} to three decimal places (0.800 vs 0.800) while incurring nearly twenty times
the carbon cost. Two models, statistically indistinguishable predictive outcomes and an
order-of-magnitude difference in ecological cost.

The explanation is algorithmic rather than incidental. Random Forest
constructs an ensemble of independently grown decision trees, evaluating
candidate splits at each node across every tree in full. XGBoost's boosted tree construction, by
contrast, uses highly-optimized histogram-based split-finding and grows trees
sequentially, each correcting the residual errors of the last. This procedure achieves equivalent
predictive capacity at a fraction of the compute. Crucially, this is the
same structural property that inflated Random Forest's tuning cost in
\Cref{sec:results_tuning}: the per-tree overhead that makes tuning expensive
makes training expensive for identical reasons and no amount of
hyperparameter optimization removes it.

% TODO: update Credit CF1 values, LR WF1, MLP CO2, XGBoost CO2 and gain magnitude after rerun
The \gls{cf1} metric captures this disproportionality directly. XGBoost \gls{cpu},
delivering equivalent accuracy at a fraction of the cost, posts the best \gls{cf1} at
0.54\,mg/F1\%. Random Forest, by contrast, finishes last with the highest \gls{cf1}
of 10.39\,mg/F1\%, the steepest single-model cost in the entire benchmark. Two results
further down the ranking are also worth noting. Logistic Regression, despite posting
the lowest predictive score on this dataset (\gls{wf1} of 0.771), ranks second in
\gls{cf1} at 0.785\,mg/F1\%: its trivially cheap training cost outweighs its
predictive disadvantage. \gls{mlp}, conversely, achieves the best \gls{wf1} at 0.804,
but emits 633\,mg CO\textsubscript{2}eq, nearly fifteen times XGBoost \gls{cpu}'s 43\,mg CO\textsubscript{2}eq,
for a predictive gain of just 0.004 points. Its \gls{cf1} (7.87\,mg/F1\%) falls well
above both XGBoost variants. The Credit ranking therefore illustrates two complementary
efficiency failure modes: the severe algorithmic overhead of Random Forest at the top
and the disproportionate cost of marginal neural-network gains in the middle.
The implication extends beyond this
dataset. When algorithm families differ structurally in computational
profile, choosing the right family is at least as consequential for
ecological efficiency as any hardware decision
\parencite{verdecchiaDataCentricGreenAI2022a}.

\paragraph{The High-Data Regime: Hardware Saturation and Role Reversals}
\label{sec:results_higgs}

Finally, as shown in the rightmost clusters of \Cref{fig:vergleich_performance}
and \Cref{fig:vergleich_efficiency}, both dimensions of the comparison shift
decisively at eleven million rows. Random Forest is excluded from this dataset
for the reasons established in \Cref{sec:results_tuning}: the per-trial training
cost made a full 40-trial HIGGS run ecologically unjustifiable.
% TODO: update HIGGS LR WF1, MLP WF1, MLP CO2, XGBoost WF1 and GPU/CPU ratio after rerun
Logistic Regression saturates at a
\gls{wf1} of 0.637, a performance ceiling that additional data cannot raise,
as the scaling analysis in \Cref{sec:results_scaling} confirms. Among
non-linear models, \gls{mlp} achieves the highest \gls{wf1} in the entire
benchmark at 0.787, but its training footprint of 145.9\,g
CO\textsubscript{2} is the most expensive single run in the study. Most
strikingly, XGBoost \gls{gpu} emits one seventh of what XGBoost \gls{cpu}
does for an equivalent \gls{wf1} of 0.760. On Wine, the \gls{gpu} was a
liability. On HIGGS, it is the ecologically dominant choice.

The reversal follows directly from the \gls{simt} execution model discussed
in \Cref{theoretical_background}. At the scale of eleven million rows, the
data volume finally saturates the \gls{gpu}'s parallel cores. The fixed costs of device
initialization and memory transfer that dominated the Wine result are now
fully amortized across the dataset. XGBoost's histogram-based tree
construction maps naturally onto this architecture, distributing
split-finding workload across thousands of cores in a way that sequential
\gls{cpu}-bound computation cannot match. Logistic Regression's
ceiling, by contrast, is independent of hardware: a linear decision boundary
cannot partition the HIGGS feature space with sufficient fidelity regardless
of the compute invested. The \gls{mlp}, meanwhile, requires iterative
backpropagation passes over eleven million samples, driving its energy
consumption to the highest single-run cost in the entire benchmark.

% TODO: update HIGGS CF1 values after rerun
The \gls{cf1} ranking on HIGGS inverts relative to raw F1. XGBoost \gls{gpu}
achieves the best \gls{cf1} at 27.7\,mg/F1\%, while \gls{mlp}, despite its predictive
advantage, finishes last at 1852.8\,mg/F1\%. The \gls{mlp}'s training footprint dwarfs
every other model on HIGGS and its predictive lead cannot compensate for it. Whether
deployment-scale inference changes this assessment is the subject of \Cref{sec:results_lifecycle}.

\paragraph{Synthesis: The Contingency of Ecological Efficiency}
\label{sec:results_main_synthesis}

No single model leads across all three datasets. Logistic Regression wins
on Wine, where a linear boundary suffices and hardware overhead dominates.
XGBoost \gls{cpu} wins on Credit, where the right algorithm family delivers
equivalent accuracy at a fraction of the cost of its nearest competitor.
XGBoost \gls{gpu} wins on HIGGS, where dataset scale finally amortizes the
fixed costs of \gls{gpu} execution.

The \gls{cf1} rankings shift substantially between datasets,
and in each case the shift is mechanistically traceable: to hardware
initialization overhead at small scale, to algorithmic structure at medium
scale and to \gls{simt} saturation at large scale. The composite metric
does not merely reorder a static ranking. It captures a different cost
regime at each level of the analysis. This is the empirical answer to the
study's primary research question. Ecological efficiency is not a property
that any model either has or lacks. It is the dynamic outcome of a specific
alignment between computational profile, hardware and task scale
\parencite{wuSustainableAIEnvironmental2022}.

\parencite{nickollsGPUComputingEra2010}. The histogram-based tree
construction distributes efficiently across thousands of cores and the
fixed memory transfer costs that dominated at small scale are now fully
amortized by the parallelization gains.

\paragraph{Synthesis: Rethinking the Big Data Dogma}
Assessed through the \gls{cf1} lens, the diverse scaling patterns compress into a single, clarifying trend: as dataset size grows, the emissions gap between hardware-aligned models and inefficient algorithms drastically widens. % TODO: update XGBoost GPU CF1 at 5M rows and XGBoost CPU comparison value after rerun

At five million rows it achieves the best \gls{cf1} against XGBoost \gls{cpu} at the same F1 of 0.757, a gap that widens further at the full eleven million row dataset.

More profoundly, these scaling curves challenge the pervasive assumption that more data is unconditionally better, which drives modern machine learning. In the pursuit of state-of-the-art accuracy, the default industrial practice is to feed algorithms the largest dataset available \parencite{schwartzGreenAI2020}. The empirical evidence here proves that this practice is often an ecological liability. At the tail end of a learning curve, forcing a model to ingest ten million additional rows to extract a marginal gain in F1-score results in a disproportionate explosion of carbon emissions. True Green \gls{ai} requires practitioners to abandon the assumption that all available data must be used. Instead, as recent data-centric \gls{ai} studies argue, we must proactively identify the threshold where predictive returns diminish \parencite{verdecchiaDataCentricGreenAI2022a}, treating dataset size not as a fixed asset, but as a hyperparameter that must be aggressively tuned to halt training before the ecological cost eclipses the scientific value.

\section{Carbon Intensity Analysis}
\label{sec:results_carbon}

The analyses conducted in the preceding sections treat CO\textsubscript{2} emissions as a fixed function of the training run: given the same model, dataset and hardware, the reported figure does not change.
This is a consequence of how CodeCarbon works: it converts measured energy into CO\textsubscript{2} using a static intensity factor of 381\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh} for Germany, a calculated average that cannot reflect when a run actually takes place (see \Cref{sec:theoretical_codecarbon}).
The German grid, however, draws on a fundamentally different fuel mix in January than in July and during peak evening demand than during solar-rich midday hours, a structure visible directly in the hourly ElectricityMaps data used in this analysis.

\centering

    \caption{January 2025 (21--30 Jan). Gas, coal and nuclear dominate, with solar generation near zero.}
    \label{fig:electricity_mix_jan}
\end{subfigure}\hspace{0.01\linewidth}\begin{subfigure}[t]{0.49\linewidth}
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{electricity_mix_germany_july.pdf}
    \caption{July 2025 (1--10 Jul). Solar becomes the dominant midday source, sharply reducing the fossil fuel share during daylight hours.}
    \label{fig:electricity_mix_jul}
\end{subfigure}
\caption{Electricity production mix in Germany for representative ten-day windows in winter and summer.}
\label{fig:electricity_mix_comparison}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:electricity_mix_jan,fig:electricity_mix_jul} illustrate this contrast directly. In January, dispatchable sources (gas, coal and nuclear) form the base of the stack, with wind providing a variable but significant contribution and solar generation remaining negligible throughout the day. In July, the picture reverses: solar becomes the dominant midday source, regularly exceeding 20{,}000\,MW, while gas and coal drop to a much smaller share. It is this structural shift in fuel composition that drives the seasonal variation in carbon intensity quantified in the analysis below.

If this temporal variation is large enough, then the timing of a training run is itself a meaningful ecological decision, one that requires no changes to code, hardware, or model architecture.
This section evaluates that hypothesis directly by counterfactually re-assigning the energy of every training run in this study to different timing scenarios derived from one year of hourly ElectricityMaps data, as described in \Cref{design_carbon_intensity}.

\FloatBarrier
\paragraph{Annual Spread and Scenario Intensities}
The first result is somewhat reassuring for the existing measurements: as shown in \Cref{fig:scenario_intensities}, the static CodeCarbon factor of 381\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh} is a defensible approximation of the annual mean, which the empirical data places at 341\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh}.
A workload executed at a uniformly random point during the year would therefore be assigned only a modest systematic error by CodeCarbon.
The more important finding is the spread around that average.
The best recorded hour of 2024 reaches 115\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh} and the worst reaches 658\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh}, a factor of 5.7$\times$ between two hours.
The absolute extremes are not practically exploitable, since no practitioner can guarantee their run coincides with the single cleanest or dirtiest hour of the year.
The structurally informed seasonal-diurnal combinations are the actionable range: summer midday hours average 193\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh} while winter evening hours average 389\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh}, a factor of two achievable through deliberate scheduling alone.

The winter window alone spans from 311 to 614\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh} across ten consecutive days, a range wider than the gap between winter and summer means.

\paragraph{Synthesis}
Taken together, these findings introduce a dimension of ecological efficiency that the main benchmark cannot capture: timing.
The static CodeCarbon factor is not grossly wrong as an annual average, but it flattens a distribution that is wide enough to change the practical interpretation of the results.
It is worth noting that this intensity overestimation of roughly 12\,\% (381 versus 341\,\gls{gco2eq}/\gls{kwh}) acts in the \textit{opposite} direction to the \gls{tdp}-based underestimation documented in \Cref{sec:results_measurement}, which inflates the corrected value by a median factor of 1.87. The intensity error is small enough that the energy-measurement error dominates in every case; the net effect of uncorrected CodeCarbon is still substantial undercounting.
The emissions reported in this study should therefore be understood as contingent on when the runs were executed, not as intrinsic properties of the algorithms.
For small datasets such as Wine and Credit, this contingency is inconsequential in absolute terms, as the runs are too cheap for timing to shift the total by a meaningful amount.
For large-scale workloads such as the \gls{mlp} on HIGGS, grid-aware scheduling would have reduced the training footprint by the equivalent of the entire Credit dataset's combined model training budget.
At that scale, scheduling is no longer a marginal consideration but a first-order efficiency lever, comparable in impact to moderate algorithmic improvements and achievable without touching a single line of model code.

evenly across the deployment period. For practitioners concerned with total
deployment footprint rather than single-phase training emissions, the
break-even point is as informative as any figure in the benchmark table.

The roughly five orders of magnitude separating these extremes reflect a structural divide rather than an accident of scale. Models with cheap training and slow inference front-load their ecological cost at the point of training, then spend that cost again with every subsequent prediction. Models with expensive training and fast inference invert this: the initial investment is large, but it is amortized across a large number of cheap queries. Which profile is ecologically preferable depends entirely on how many predictions the deployed system will serve, a figure that has nothing to do with accuracy or training efficiency \parencite{luccioniPowerHungryProcessing2024}.

This creates a direct inversion of the training-only rankings for high-volume deployments \parencite{desislavovTrendsAIInference2023}. Random Forest on Wine achieves a favorable \gls{cf1} precisely because it is cheap to train. Yet at ten thousand predictions it has already accumulated roughly 95 times its training budget in inference emissions, while \gls{mlp} at the same query count has barely touched its much larger training investment. For a practitioner deploying at scale, the \gls{cf1} ranking is not merely incomplete at this point. It points in the wrong direction. Ecological model selection therefore requires a deployment volume estimate as an explicit input. Below the break-even threshold, optimizing training emissions is the right lever. Above it, inference latency per prediction governs the total lifecycle cost and no amount of training efficiency compensates for a slow prediction function at scale.

\section{Chapter Summary}
\label{sec:results_summary}

Across the eight analyses in this chapter, the empirical answer to the core research question is conditional rather than absolute. % TODO: update CF1 values in chapter summary after rerun
On the \gls{cf1} metric, Logistic Regression leads on Wine, XGBoost \gls{cpu} on Credit and XGBoost \gls{gpu} on HIGGS. No single model occupies all three positions because ecological efficiency depends entirely on the fit between model capacity, hardware utilization and task complexity. Logistic Regression is efficient where a linear decision boundary is sufficient. XGBoost \gls{gpu} is efficient where the massive parallelism of \gls{gpu}-accelerated tree construction finally outweighs its fixed overhead. The rankings are dataset-contingent by design, demonstrating empirically that any claim that one classifier is categorically more ecological than another must strictly specify the scale and hardware context in which that claim is meant to hold.

More profoundly, the full accounting of the machine learning pipeline shifts this picture further in every dimension examined, exposing the severe ecological limitations of standard, training-only benchmarking. % TODO: update tuning/training cost multiplier for MLP HIGGS after rerun
Hyperparameter search adds a one-time overhead that can exceed the training benchmark cost by more than an order of magnitude, as the \gls{mlp} tuning run on HIGGS cost roughly 15 times the benchmark run it enabled. Grid carbon intensity introduces a factor-of-two scaling on effective emissions that depends on temporal scheduling alone, entirely independent of any modelling decision. Finally, lifecycle analysis distributes the total cost across the deployment period in ways that vary by roughly five orders of magnitude across model-dataset combinations.

A practitioner targeting ecological efficiency in tabular classification therefore cannot optimize training emissions in isolation. Model choice, hardware, tuning strategy, scheduling and deployment volume each contribute a distinct lever and they interact dynamically. The ultimate takeaway of this chapter is that true Green \gls{ai} requires abandoning the narrow evaluation of algorithms in a computational vacuum. \Cref{conclusion} draws on this combined, systemic picture to formulate actionable recommendations for each of these levers and to definitively answer the research questions stated in \Cref{sec:research_questions}.
