% ── HIGGS Scaling ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{HIGGS Dataset Scaling}
\label{sec:results_scaling}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{scaling_higgs.pdf}
\caption{All models evaluated on different scales of the HIGGS subsets.}
\label{fig:scaling_higgs}
\end{figure}

\Cref{fig:scaling_higgs} shows CO\textsubscript{2} emissions, training time, and weighted F1 as a function of dataset size across nine HIGGS subsets ranging from 1{,}000 to 11{,}000{,}000 rows. The following observations summarise the key findings.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Random Forest has the highest emissions of all models, despite its conceptual simplicity.}
        At 500{,}000 rows, Random Forest emits 8.39\,mg\,CO\textsubscript{2}, compared to 2.75\,mg for MLP and 0.50\,mg for XGBoost CPU.
        It was excluded from runs above 500{,}000 rows due to prohibitive training time, meaning its full scaling behaviour is not visible in the plot.
        The underlying reason is architectural: training an ensemble of independent decision trees requires sequential or coarse-grained parallel tree construction, which does not map efficiently onto GPU acceleration and scales super-linearly with dataset size.
        This result illustrates that ecological cost is not determined by model complexity in the abstract sense, but by how well a model's training procedure exploits available hardware parallelism.

  \item \textbf{At 500{,}000 rows, MLP achieves a higher F1-score than Random Forest while emitting less than one third of its CO\textsubscript{2}.}
        MLP reaches a weighted F1 of 0.745 against Random Forest's 0.732, at 2.75\,mg versus 8.39\,mg CO\textsubscript{2}.
        The model that is architecturally more complex therefore dominates on both axes simultaneously at this scale.

  \item \textbf{XGBoost GPU is the most emissions-efficient model at large scale.}
        At 5{,}000{,}000 rows it emits 0.98\,mg CO\textsubscript{2} while matching XGBoost CPU in F1 (0.757 vs.\ 0.757) at roughly one sixth of its emissions.
        At 11{,}000{,}000 rows the gap widens further: XGBoost GPU emits 2.10\,mg while XGBoost CPU emits 14.65\,mg for identical predictive performance.

  \item \textbf{Logistic Regression shows near-flat accuracy above approximately 10{,}000 rows.}
        Weighted F1 improves from 0.626 at 1{,}000 rows to 0.637 at 100{,}000 rows, and does not improve further at any larger subset size.
        Additional data therefore yields no accuracy return for this model on HIGGS, making any compute spent beyond 100{,}000 rows ecologically wasteful relative to the gain.

  \item \textbf{Accuracy gains diminish for all models above 1{,}000{,}000 rows.}
        XGBoost CPU improves from 0.746 at 1\,M rows to 0.757 at 5\,M rows and 0.760 at 11\,M rows, a gain of 0.014 over a tenfold increase in data.
        MLP improves from 0.755 at 1\,M to 0.787 at 11\,M rows, the largest absolute gain among the remaining models, but at a CO\textsubscript{2} cost that grows disproportionately: emissions increase by a factor of 28 over the same range while F1 improves by 4 percentage points.
\end{itemize}

% ── MLP Architecture Variation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{MLP Architecture Variation}
\label{sec:results_mlp_arch}

\Cref{fig:mlp_variation} shows weighted F1 and CO\textsubscript{2} emissions for all twelve MLP configurations (depth $\in \{1,2,3,4\}$, width $\in \{128, 256, 512\}$) trained on the full HIGGS dataset.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{mlp_variation.pdf}
\caption{MLP architecture variation on HIGGS: weighted F1 (left) and CO\textsubscript{2} emissions (right) for all depth-width combinations. Darker cells indicate higher values.}
\label{fig:mlp_variation}
\end{figure}

Width has a stronger effect on predictive performance than depth. Moving from 128 to 512 units per layer increases F1 by roughly 0.04 across all depth levels, whereas adding layers beyond two yields gains below 0.01. The F1 ceiling is reached early: the 2$\times$512 architecture already achieves a weighted F1 of 0.775, only 0.007 below the best-observed configuration (4$\times$512, F1\,=\,0.782).

The CO\textsubscript{2} picture is more nuanced. For narrow networks (width\,=\,128), emissions \textit{decrease} with depth, falling from 0.129\,kg at depth~1 to 0.071\,kg at depth~3, before recovering slightly to 0.074\,kg at depth~4. This counterintuitive pattern reflects early stopping: deeper narrow networks appear to converge in fewer epochs on HIGGS, so the total compute per training run is lower despite the larger parameter count. For wider networks this effect disappears; at width\,=\,512, emissions increase monotonically with depth from 0.144\,kg to 0.310\,kg.

Taken together, the results identify 2$\times$512 as a practically efficient architecture: it captures 98.2\,\% of the best-observed F1 at 58\,\% of its CO\textsubscript{2} cost (0.181\,kg vs.\ 0.310\,kg). Configurations with three or four layers offer only marginal predictive improvements while incurring substantially higher emissions, illustrating the diminishing-returns structure that motivates architecture search from an ecological standpoint.

\section{Carbon Intensity Analysis}
\label{sec:results_carbon}

The analyses conducted in the preceding sections treat CO\textsubscript{2} emissions as a fixed function of the training run: given the same model, dataset, and hardware, the reported figure does not change.
This is a consequence of how CodeCarbon works: it converts measured energy into CO\textsubscript{2} using a static intensity factor of 381\,gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh for Germany, a calculated average that cannot reflect when a run actually takes place.
The German grid, however, draws on a fundamentally different fuel mix in January than in July, and during peak evening demand than during solar-rich midday hours, a structure visible directly in the hourly ElectricityMaps data used in this analysis.
If this temporal variation is large enough, then the timing of a training run is itself a meaningful ecological decision, one that requires no changes to code, hardware, or model architecture.
This section evaluates that hypothesis directly by counterfactually re-assigning the energy of every training run in this study to different timing scenarios derived from one year of hourly ElectricityMaps data, as described in \Cref{design_carbon_intensity}.

The first result is somewhat reassuring for the existing measurements: as shown in \Cref{fig:scenario_intensities}, the static CodeCarbon factor of 381\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh is a defensible approximation of the annual mean, which the empirical data places at 341\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh.
A workload executed at a uniformly random point during the year would therefore be assigned only a modest systematic error by CodeCarbon.
The more important finding is the spread around that average.
The best recorded hour of 2024 reaches 115\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh, and the worst reaches 658\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh, a factor of 5.7$\times$ between two hours.
The absolute extremes are not practically exploitable, since no practitioner can guarantee their run coincides with the single cleanest or dirtiest hour of the year.
The structurally informed seasonal-diurnal combinations are the actionable range: summer midday hours average 193\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh while winter evening hours average 389\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh, a factor of two achievable through deliberate scheduling alone.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{scenario_intensities.pdf}
\caption{Carbon intensity (gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh) for eight timing scenarios}
\label{fig:scenario_intensities}
\end{figure}

Because training energy is a fixed physical quantity for any given run, every percentage difference in grid intensity translates directly into the same percentage difference in CO\textsubscript{2}.
\Cref{fig:scenario_per_run} expresses this proportionality relative to the static CodeCarbon baseline, making the stakes concrete.
Scheduling during summer mean conditions would reduce the reported emissions of this study by approximately 20\,\% compared to the static estimate, requiring no modification to the experimental setup whatsoever.
The best-hour scenario pushes this reduction to 70\,\%, while the worst-hour scenario reverses it into a 73\,\% increase.
These percentages are identical for every model and dataset, because the intensity factor scales uniformly; what differs is the absolute magnitude of the effect.
For lightweight runs such as Logistic Regression on Wine, the difference between any two scenarios is negligible in absolute terms.
For the MLP trained on HIGGS, the gap between best-hour and worst-hour timing amounts to approximately 208\,g\,CO\textsubscript{2}, a quantity that exceeds the total training footprint of every Wine and Credit run in this study combined.
This asymmetry reveals an important practical principle: grid-aware scheduling is most valuable precisely where algorithmic and hardware efficiency decisions also matter most, namely on large, compute-intensive workloads where the absolute energy expenditure is high enough for timing to make a meaningful difference.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{scenario_per_run.pdf}
\caption{Counterfactual CO\textsubscript{2} relative to the static CodeCarbon baseline (= 100\,\%)}
\label{fig:scenario_per_run}
\end{figure}

To understand why the diurnal dimension matters as much as the seasonal one, consider the solar duck curve visible in \Cref{fig:carbon_intensity_july_day} for 24 July 2024.
Carbon intensity declines from roughly 300\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh in the morning as solar generation ramps up, reaches a minimum of 163\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh near midday, then recovers sharply to a peak of 461\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh once solar output falls in the evening.
The static CodeCarbon factor of 381\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh intersects this curve only during the early morning and late evening hours, the two dirtiest windows of the day on a summer date with strong solar generation.
During the core daylight hours, the static factor overestimates the actual intensity by up to a factor of two.
This is not an anomaly specific to this particular date: it is the structural consequence of Germany's growing solar capacity, which consistently depresses midday intensity throughout the summer months.
A practitioner who runs training jobs during office hours on summer days is inadvertently benefiting from this effect, while their CodeCarbon reports attribute a higher carbon cost than actually occurred.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{carbon_intensity_july_day.pdf}
\caption{Hourly carbon intensity on 24 July 2024 in Germany}
\label{fig:carbon_intensity_july_day}
\end{figure}

The seasonal dimension adds a second layer of variation on top of the diurnal one.
The ten-day windows shown in \Cref{fig:carbon_intensity_seasonal} reveal that winter and autumn both sit above the static CodeCarbon factor on average, meaning CodeCarbon systematically \textit{underestimates} winter emissions rather than overestimating them.
The winter window averages 474\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh, nearly 25\,\% above the static factor, while spring (347\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh) and summer (361\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh) fall meaningfully below it.
This directional asymmetry matters for transparency: the runs in this study were conducted during periods of varying grid intensity, and the static factor provides no indication of whether a given run's reported footprint is an over- or underestimate.
An equally important finding is that within-season variance is large enough to undermine coarse seasonal scheduling as a reliable strategy.
The winter window alone spans from 311 to 614\,gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh across ten consecutive days, a range wider than the gap between winter and summer means.
A practitioner who decides to schedule training in July rather than January improves their expected footprint, but cannot assume low intensity on any given July day without consulting real-time grid data.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{carbon_intensity_seasonal.pdf}
\caption{Hourly carbon intensity for representative ten-day windows per season, Germany 2025}
\label{fig:carbon_intensity_seasonal}
\end{figure}

The year-long heatmap in \Cref{fig:diurnal_seasonal} brings both dimensions together and makes their joint structure immediately legible.
The lowest-intensity region of the entire year is summer midday: a consistent band of green running from May through August between approximately 10:00 and 15:00, reflecting the hours when solar generation is both abundant and at its seasonal peak.
The highest-intensity region is winter evenings: the band of red and orange concentrated between 17:00 and 21:00 from November through February, when solar output has already dropped to near zero and space heating drives gas and coal generation.
What the heatmap makes clear is that these two extremes are not separated by chance; they are structurally determined by the interaction of Germany's solar build-out with the seasonal daylight cycle and heating demand patterns.
A practitioner optimizing scheduling on local infrastructure is therefore not searching for a random low-intensity moment; they are navigating a predictable structure where the best and worst times are largely known in advance.
The summer-midday to winter-evening gradient spans the full two-to-one ratio identified earlier, and represents the maximum emissions reduction achievable through scheduling on the German grid without any hardware or algorithmic changes.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{diurnal_seasonal.pdf}
\caption{Mean carbon intensity (gCO\textsubscript{2}/kWh) by hour of day and calendar month for the German grid (2024), derived from 8{,}760 hourly observations.}
\label{fig:diurnal_seasonal}
\end{figure}

Taken together, these findings introduce a dimension of ecological efficiency that the main benchmark cannot capture: timing.
The static CodeCarbon factor is not grossly wrong as an annual average, but it flattens a distribution that is wide enough to change the practical interpretation of the results.
The emissions reported in this study should therefore be understood as contingent on when the runs were executed, not as intrinsic properties of the algorithms.
For small datasets such as Wine and Credit, this contingency is inconsequential in absolute terms, as the runs are too cheap for timing to shift the total by a meaningful amount.
For large-scale workloads such as the MLP on HIGGS, grid-aware scheduling would have reduced the training footprint by the equivalent of the entire Credit dataset's combined model training budget.
At that scale, scheduling is no longer a marginal consideration but a first-order efficiency lever, comparable in impact to moderate algorithmic improvements, and achievable without touching a single line of model code.
