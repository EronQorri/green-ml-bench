\chapter{Related Work}
\label{related_work}

This chapter surveys empirical studies that have measured the energy consumption and carbon emissions of machine learning systems. The focus is on what prior work has concretely found, not on the underlying concepts, which are established in \Cref{theoretical_background}. Each section closes by identifying what prior studies leave unmeasured or unresolved, collectively motivating the specific design choices of this study.

\section{Empirical Energy Benchmarks for Machine Learning}
\label{sec:related_benchmarks}

As the field of machine learning has matured, the evaluation of models has increasingly shifted from theoretical complexity analyses to empirical benchmarking. A review of the existing literature reveals, however, that these benchmarks typically isolate either the ecological costs of deep learning or the predictive accuracy of tabular data models, rarely examining the intersection of both.

The most rigorous empirical energy benchmarks to date have focused exclusively on large-scale deep learning systems. \citeauthor{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019} \cite{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019} benchmarked the training energy of various NLP models by directly measuring hardware interfaces, highlighting the massive carbon footprint of architectures like BERT and helping to spark the Green AI movement. Building on this, \citeauthor{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} \cite{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} measured the carbon intensity of AI training across cloud environments, demonstrating that hardware efficiency and grid carbon intensity drastically alter the environmental impact of large neural networks, while \citeauthor{desislavovTrendsAIInference2023} \cite{desislavovTrendsAIInference2023} extended this empirical scope to the inference phase, compiling a large dataset of energy consumption trends for vision and language models. A notable step away from these Google-scale architectures was taken by \citeauthor{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020} \cite{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020}, who developed the \textit{experiment-impact-tracker} framework and empirically benchmarked Reinforcement Learning algorithms on standard CPUs and GPUs, demonstrating that even relatively ``small'' training runs generate clearly measurable emissions. This establishes ecological efficiency as a concern for everyday experiments rather than exclusively for massive architectures, yet the focus of all these studies remains firmly on deep neural networks applied to unstructured data.

Empirical benchmarks on structured, tabular data tell a different story: they have traditionally prioritized predictive performance while leaving energy entirely unmeasured. \citeauthor{grinsztajnWhyTreebasedModels2022a} \cite{grinsztajnWhyTreebasedModels2022a} compiled 45 tabular datasets across diverse domains to benchmark tree-based models against deep neural networks, demonstrating that classical algorithms such as Random Forest and XGBoost consistently outperform deep learning on such data. \citeauthor{gorishniyRevisitingDeepLearning2021a} \cite{gorishniyRevisitingDeepLearning2021a} conducted a similar large-scale benchmark, reiterating the strong performance of gradient boosting systems. Both studies evaluated models solely on predictive accuracy and basic computational time, leaving physical energy consumption and resulting carbon footprints entirely unmeasured.

Only recently have a small number of studies begun to close this gap. \citeauthor{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024} \cite{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024} evaluated software-based energy tracking tools against physical hardware meters and, as part of their methodology, performed a limited evaluation of classical machine learning algorithms alongside deep learning models. \citeauthor{verdecchiaDataCentricGreenAI2022a} \cite{verdecchiaDataCentricGreenAI2022a} took an exploratory empirical approach to data-centric Green AI, using software trackers to measure the energy consumed by data preprocessing operations for traditional classifiers such as Support Vector Machines and Random Forests. While these studies lay important groundwork, they do not provide a comprehensive analysis of how scaling dataset sizes affects the efficiency of classical ML models.

The existing literature therefore reveals a distinct research gap. The most thorough Green AI benchmarks focus heavily on deep learning applied to unstructured data \cite{strubellEnergyPolicyConsiderations2019, dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022}, while comprehensive benchmarks on tabular datasets focus almost entirely on predictive accuracy \cite{grinsztajnWhyTreebasedModels2022a, gorishniyRevisitingDeepLearning2021a}. There is a lack of systematic, empirical benchmarks evaluating the intersection of these two domains: how energy consumption, carbon footprint, and predictive accuracy scale together as dataset sizes increase for classical classification algorithms. The present thesis addresses this gap by empirically investigating the efficiency-performance trade-offs of classical algorithms across varying tabular dataset sizes, aiming to identify where simpler, less energy-intensive models become ecologically and practically advantageous.

\section{CPU vs.\ GPU Efficiency: Empirical Evidence}
\label{sec:related_cpu_gpu}

The fundamental trade-off between Central Processing Units (CPUs) and Graphics Processing Units (GPUs) in machine learning revolves around the relationship between power draw and execution speed. To determine which hardware is truly more energy-efficient, it is not enough to look at instantaneous power consumption alone, as one must also account for how fast the hardware completes the training task.

Empirical measurements clearly show that GPUs are significantly more power-hungry than traditional CPUs. \citeauthor{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} \cite{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} report that GPUs typically consume between 250\,W and 350\,W under load, whereas CPUs generally operate in a much lower range of 10\,W to 150\,W. In a controlled experiment measuring individual hardware components during the training of a BERT language model, which is representative of high-utilization deep learning tasks, they demonstrated that the GPU alone accounted for 74\% of the total electricity consumption. Because of this substantial baseline power draw, a GPU must complete a training task significantly faster than a CPU to actually save energy overall. For highly complex deep learning architectures applied to unstructured data such as text or images, this rapid acceleration usually compensates for the higher wattage, making the GPU the more efficient choice.

However, the dynamic becomes considerably more complex when shifting the focus to classical machine learning algorithms applied to tabular data, such as Gradient Boosted Decision Trees. A foundational empirical study by \citeauthor{mitchellAcceleratingXGBoostAlgorithm2017} \cite{mitchellAcceleratingXGBoostAlgorithm2017} investigated the acceleration of the XGBoost algorithm using GPU computing. Their results demonstrated that training XGBoost on a GPU (NVIDIA Titan X) achieved speedups of 3$\times$ to 6$\times$ compared to a standard 4-core desktop CPU (Intel i7). Even when compared to a server setup utilizing two Intel Xeon CPUs with 24 cores combined, the single GPU still provided a 1.2$\times$ speedup.

While these figures confirm that GPUs can substantially accelerate classical algorithms on tabular datasets, they reveal a critical gap in the current literature: \citeauthor{mitchellAcceleratingXGBoostAlgorithm2017} \cite{mitchellAcceleratingXGBoostAlgorithm2017} exclusively measured computational speed, completely omitting physical energy consumption. As established by \citeauthor{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} \cite{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022}, a GPU draws vastly more power than a CPU. If a GPU trains a model three times faster but consumes five times more watts during that period, the overall energy efficiency of the GPU is ultimately worse than that of the CPU.

The assumption that GPUs are inherently the more energy-efficient choice is empirically well-supported for massive deep learning tasks, but it remains unproven for algorithms like XGBoost on tabular data. It is currently unclear whether the GPU's execution speedup in Gradient Boosting actually compensates for its power draw, or at what dataset size a potential break-even point occurs.

\section{Measurement Tool Accuracy: Software Estimation vs.\ Hardware Sensors}
\label{sec:related_measurement}

As the focus on Green AI intensifies, the necessity for accurate energy measurement has led to the development of various tracking tools. However, evaluating the energy consumption of a machine learning task is heavily dependent on the chosen measurement methodology. \citeauthor{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024} \cite{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024} categorize the available approaches into three main groups: physical measurements using External Power Meters (EPMs) at the wall outlet, on-chip sensors embedded in processors (such as Intel's RAPL and NVIDIA's NVML), and software estimation models. Estimation models can further be divided into data-based models and analytical models that rely on heuristics like Thermal Design Power (TDP) or execution time. While EPMs provide the absolute ground truth for total system consumption, they are expensive to deploy at scale and lack the fine-grained resolution needed to isolate the energy used by specific software processes. Consequently, researchers increasingly rely on software trackers, though empirical studies reveal severe discrepancies among them.

When multiple software tools are used to track the same machine learning experiment, the reported energy and carbon metrics can vary drastically. In a comparative study of NLP algorithms, \citeauthor{bannourEvaluatingCarbonFootprint2021} \cite{bannourEvaluatingCarbonFootprint2021} evaluated six different tracking tools, including ML CO\textsubscript{2} Impact, CarbonTracker, Experiment-Impact-Tracker, and Green Algorithms. They discovered that the CO\textsubscript{2} equivalent emissions reported for the exact same training run differed significantly depending on the tool used. Specifically, tools that rely on user-supplied information and abstract analytical models, such as Green Algorithms and ML CO\textsubscript{2} Impact, systematically overestimated energy consumption. Conversely, tools that interface directly with hardware sensors, such as CarbonTracker and Experiment-Impact-Tracker, produced more consistent results because they bypass abstract estimations in favor of actual power draw.

The fundamental weakness of analytical estimation models is their inability to capture the real-world, dynamic fluctuations of computer hardware. A common analytical approach is to estimate energy by multiplying the TDP of a CPU or GPU by the total execution time and an assumed utilization factor. However, as demonstrated by \citeauthor{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020} \cite{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020}, relying on partial information like TDP leads to severe inaccuracies. Their empirical evaluation showed that TDP-based estimates significantly over- or underestimate the actual energy consumed compared to direct sensor readings, because they ignore dynamic memory effects and real-time utilization shifts. Similarly, estimating energy based purely on Floating Point Operations (FLOPs) can be highly misleading. As empirically demonstrated by \citeauthor{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020} \cite{hendersonSystematicReportingEnergy2020}, theoretical operation counts often show little to no correlation with actual energy consumption when comparing across different model architectures, because they fail to account for underlying firmware optimizations and hardware-specific behaviors. This was practically confirmed by \citeauthor{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024} \cite{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024}, who found that pure analytical estimations were consistently far from the actual measured reference, whereas tools utilizing on-chip sensors provided estimates closer to the true dynamic energy consumption, though still subject to platform-specific limitations.

Recent research has begun to explicitly validate widely adopted software trackers against physical ground-truth measurements. \citeauthor{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} \cite{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} conducted an extensive empirical study comparing CodeCarbon's dynamic tracking against external power meters, demonstrating that CodeCarbon consistently underestimates total system power draw by 20 to 30\%, as software-based profilers cannot monitor hardware components such as cooling overhead and power supply inefficiencies. Most critically, the estimation errors are considerably larger for CPU-only workloads than for GPU workloads: in multiple configurations, the relative estimation error during CPU-only execution exceeded that of GPU execution by more than an order of magnitude. This finding has direct implications for any study relying on CodeCarbon to benchmark classical machine learning algorithms, which are predominantly CPU-bound.

While tools like CodeCarbon provide an accessible interface for tracking energy, their accuracy is further constrained by operating system permissions and hardware compatibility. Reading data directly from on-chip sensors like Intel's RAPL typically requires specific Linux kernels and administrative access. When these conditions are unavailable, such as on standard Windows machines without administrative overrides, CodeCarbon falls back to TDP-based analytical estimation, compounding the inaccuracies documented above. Existing validation studies, including \citeauthor{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} \cite{fischerGroundTruthingAIEnergy2025} and \citeauthor{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024} \cite{rodriguezEvaluatingEnergyConsumption2024}, have established these limitations in the context of deep learning and vision models. Whether comparable discrepancies arise for classical machine learning algorithms on tabular data under this specific fallback regime has not been empirically established, representing the gap this thesis addresses.

\section{Seasonal and Geographic Carbon Intensity Effects}
\label{sec:related_carbon_intensity}

While algorithmic efficiency and hardware choices are critical for Green AI, the actual ecological footprint of a machine learning experiment is ultimately dictated by where and when the computation takes place. Because the fuel mix of electricity grids varies drastically across the world and throughout the day, geographic and temporal factors have become a major focus of recent empirical research.

The physical location of the server executing a machine learning model is one of the most significant determinants of its carbon footprint. An empirical investigation by \citeauthor{lacosteQuantifyingCarbonEmissions2019} \cite{lacosteQuantifyingCarbonEmissions2019} demonstrated this variability: servers located in regions powered by hydroelectricity, such as Quebec, Canada, emit approximately 20 gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh, whereas servers in fossil-fuel-dependent regions like Iowa, USA, emit up to 736.6 gCO\textsubscript{2}eq/kWh. This represents a factor of nearly 40 difference in carbon emissions for the exact same computational workload. Further empirical evaluations by \citeauthor{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} \cite{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} confirmed this across 16 different cloud computing regions, showing that selecting the least carbon-intensive data center can reduce the emissions of training a model from 26,000 grams to 7,000 grams of CO\textsubscript{2}. The practical implication for experiment design is clear: because model training is generally not latency-bound, practitioners can substantially reduce their emissions by shifting workloads to carbon-friendly regions. Tools such as Green Algorithms \cite{lannelongueGreenAlgorithmsQuantifying2021} and the ML CO\textsubscript{2} Impact Calculator \cite{lacosteQuantifyingCarbonEmissions2019} have been introduced specifically to help researchers account for these regional differences during experiment planning.

Beyond geography, the carbon footprint of an AI model is also subject to temporal fluctuations, as the availability of renewable energy sources changes over time. \citeauthor{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} \cite{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} empirically measured diurnal variations and found them to play a meaningful role: because solar energy generation peaks during the day, starting a model training run at midnight instead of 6:00 AM can increase carbon emissions by up to 8\% in certain regions. They propose shifting workloads in time, either by delaying the start of an experiment or dynamically pausing and resuming training based on real-time grid emissions. When examining \textit{seasonal} variations across different months, however, the picture differs. In their global analysis of cloud instances tracked over a 12-month period, \citeauthor{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} \cite{dodgeMeasuringCarbonIntensity2022} found the trends to be relatively flat, concluding that emissions within a single cloud region remain broadly similar across months.

This conclusion regarding negligible seasonal variation may, however, be strongly tied to the specific infrastructure of massive, globally distributed cloud providers. Local electricity grids with a high penetration of seasonally dependent renewables, such as the German public grid, which relies heavily on solar power in the summer and a higher share of fossil fuels in the winter, may exhibit considerably stronger seasonal effects that are not captured in global cloud averages. No existing study has conducted an empirical machine learning benchmark to directly compare the energy and carbon footprint of model training on the German grid between months with vastly different renewable energy shares.
